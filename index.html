Glimpse of Old Toronto

History

Campbell House Museum 1822

Campbell House was built in 1822 for Chief Justice William Campbell and it is one of the oldest surviving buildings in early Toronto, which was then called York. The house is designed in the Georgian style and reflects the life of upper-class families in the early 19th century. In the 1970s, the entire building was moved from its original location to prevent demolition, allowing it to be preserved as a museum today.

Montgomery's Inn 1830

Montgomery’s Inn is a historic inn and museum located in the Etobicoke area of Toronto, originally built in 1830 by Irish immigrant Thomas Montgomery. Once a busy stop along Dundas Street, the inn served travelers with food, lodging, and community gathering space during a time when this route was a key connection into the growing town of York (now Toronto). Today, the restored building operates as a museum that showcases early 19th-century life, complete with period rooms, cooking hearths, and exhibits on local history. Visitors can explore how inns functioned as social and economic hubs, offering insight into travel, trade, and everyday life in early Upper Canada.

Colborne Lodge 1837

Colborne Lodge is a historic home located in High Park. Built by John George Howard, the first professional architect in Toronto, it serves as a museum showcasing the early history of the city and the Howard family's contributions to Toronto's development. The house retains many original furnishings and offers views of the park's picturesque ponds.

Gibson House Museums 1851

The Gibson House was built in 1851 for David Gibson, a Scottish immigrant, land surveyor, and political reformer. The house represents rural life in early Toronto during the mid-19th century. Today the restored home shows how families lived and worked in the growing community that later became North York.

Mackenzie House 1858

Mackenzie House is a historic 19th-century townhouse located on Bond Street in downtown Toronto. Built in 1858, it was the final home of William Lyon Mackenzie, the city’s first mayor and a key figure in the 1837 Upper Canada Rebellion. Today, the house operates as a museum that recreates Victorian-era urban life, featuring period furnishings, a working printing press, and artifacts connected to Mackenzie’s work as a journalist and political reformer. Saved from demolition in the 1930s, the preserved building offers insight into early Toronto history, highlighting both domestic life and the development of democratic ideas in Canada.

Spadina House 1866

Spadina Museum was originally built in 1866 by financier James Austin. The house was expanded and renovated in the early 20th century, reflecting the style and lifestyle of Toronto’s wealthy families during the Edwardian era. The museum today recreates how the Austin family lived between 1900 and the 1930s.

The Keg Mansion 1867

The Keg Mansion was originally built in 1867 as Euclid Hall for Hart Massey, a wealthy industrialist from the Massey manufacturing family. The large Victorian house later became a restaurant and is now one of the most famous historic dining locations in Toronto. The building is also known for stories and legends about it being haunted.

George Brown House 1874-1876

The George Brown House, also known as Lambton Lodge, is a historic mansion in Toronto built between 1874 and 1876 in the Second Empire style. It was the home of George Brown, a key Father of Confederation and founder of The Globe. Located on Beverley Street, the house features a distinctive mansard roof, red brick façade, and richly detailed interiors. It holds national historic significance for its connection to Canadian politics and the anti-slavery movement, and today it serves as an event and office space preserved by the Ontario Heritage Trust.

Casa Lomas 1911-1914

Casa Loma was built between 1911 and 1914 for Sir Henry Pellatt, a wealthy financier and military officer. Designed in a Gothic Revival style, the building resembles a European castle with towers, secret passages, and large gardens. Today Casa Loma is one of Toronto’s most famous landmarks and a major historic attraction.

Black Creek Pioneer Village 1960

Black Creek Pioneer Village is a living history museum located in Toronto that recreates rural life in 19th-century Ontario. Opened in 1960, the village features over 40 historic buildings—including homes, a schoolhouse, a church, and workshops—that were either preserved or relocated to the site. Costumed interpreters demonstrate traditional trades such as blacksmithing, baking, and weaving, allowing visitors to experience how people lived and worked during the 1800s. The village offers an immersive environment that highlights early settler life, community structures, and the development of agriculture and industry in Upper Canada.

About the Project

TORONTO HISTORICAL HOMES ARCHIVE is a project that explores a selection of historic mansions across the city, tracing their origins, architecture, and the lives once lived within them. Each house represents a different moment in Toronto’s development, revealing how spaces of living, wealth, and identity have changed over time.

Through a combination of map, timeline, and visual storytelling, the project brings these sites together as a connected narrative. Rather than viewing them as isolated landmarks, it presents them as part of a larger story—one that reflects Toronto’s layered history and the traces it leaves behind in the built environment.
